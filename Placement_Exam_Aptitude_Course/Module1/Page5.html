Profit and Loss

Concept Overview

Profit and Loss is the most practical application of percentages. It governs all commercial transactions. Understanding the relationships between Cost Price, Selling Price, and Marked Price is essential for solving problems on trade, discounts, and markups.

1. Basic Terminology

  • Cost Price (CP): The price at which goods are purchased.
  • Selling Price (SP): The price at which goods are sold.
  • Marked Price (MP) / List Price: The price printed on the tag or label.
  • Profit (Gain): SP > CP. Profit = SP - CP.
  • Loss: CP > SP. Loss = CP - SP.

2. Profit and Loss Percentage

Profit and Loss are always calculated on the Cost Price (CP) unless specified otherwise.

  • Profit % = (Profit / CP) × 100
  • Loss % = (Loss / CP) × 100

3. Discount and Marked Price

Discount is always calculated on the Marked Price (MP).

  • Discount = MP - SP
  • Discount % = (Discount / MP) × 100
  • SP = MP × (1 - Discount%/100)

4. Successive Discounts and Markup

Successive Discounts

When multiple discounts are given one after another, they are calculated on the diminishing value.

Formula: Single equivalent discount for d1% and d2% = (d1 + d2 - (d1×d2)/100)%.

Markup

Markup is the amount by which the price is increased above the CP to arrive at the MP.

Markup % = ((MP - CP) / CP) × 100

Detailed Explanation: Dishonest Dealer

A dishonest dealer uses false weights to cheat customers. He claims to sell at CP but uses a weight of W grams instead of 1000 grams (1 kg).

Gain % = (Error / (True Value - Error)) × 100

Real-World Examples

Scenario: Retail Pricing Strategy

A retailer buys a shirt for $400. He wants a 20% profit after giving a 10% discount. What should be the marked price?

Solution: Let MP be x.

SP after 10% discount = 0.9x.

Desired Profit = 20% on CP of $400 -> SP = 400 × 1.2 = $480.

Equating SPs: 0.9x = 480.

x = 480 / 0.9 = $533.33.
