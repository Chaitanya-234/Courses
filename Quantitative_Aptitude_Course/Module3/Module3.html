3.1 Algebra: The Language of Mathematics

Algebra uses symbols and letters to represent numbers and quantities in formulas and equations. It provides a powerful framework for solving problems systematically and generalizing mathematical relationships.

Linear Equations

A linear equation in one variable has the form ax + b = 0, where a ≠ 0. The solution is x = -b/a.

Solving Linear Equations: Isolate the variable by performing the same operations on both sides. Example: 3x + 7 = 22 → 3x = 15 → x = 5

Linear Equations in Two Variables: Equations like 2x + 3y = 12 have infinitely many solutions. Two such equations form a system that can be solved using:

  • Substitution Method: Solve one equation for one variable, substitute into the other
  • Elimination Method: Add or subtract equations to eliminate one variable
  • Cross-Multiplication Method: For equations a₁x + b₁y = c₁ and a₂x + b₂y = c₂

Quadratic Equations

A quadratic equation has the form ax² + bx + c = 0, where a ≠ 0. Solutions can be found using:

Factorization: Express as (px + q)(rx + s) = 0, then px + q = 0 or rx + s = 0

Quadratic Formula: x = [-b ± √(b² - 4ac)] / 2a

Discriminant (D = b² - 4ac): Determines nature of roots:

  • D > 0: Two distinct real roots
  • D = 0: Two equal real roots (one repeated root)
  • D < 0: No real roots (complex roots)

Polynomials

A polynomial is an expression consisting of variables and coefficients involving only addition, subtraction, multiplication, and non-negative integer exponents.

Degree: The highest power of the variable. Example: 3x⁴ + 2x² - 5x + 7 has degree 4

Operations: Polynomials can be added, subtracted, multiplied, and divided (with remainder)

Inequalities

Inequalities use symbols <, >, ≤, ≥ to compare expressions. Solving inequalities follows similar rules to equations, with one critical exception: multiplying or dividing by a negative number reverses the inequality sign.

Example: -2x + 6 > 10 → -2x > 4 → x < -2 (sign reversed when dividing by -2)

Basic Algebraic Identities

  • (a + b)² = a² + 2ab + b²
  • (a - b)² = a² - 2ab + b²
  • a² - b² = (a + b)(a - b)
  • (a + b)³ = a³ + 3a²b + 3ab² + b³
  • (a - b)³ = a³ - 3a²b + 3ab² - b³
  • a³ + b³ = (a + b)(a² - ab + b²)
  • a³ - b³ = (a - b)(a² + ab + b²)

3.2 Time and Work

Time and work problems involve calculating how long it takes individuals or groups to complete tasks, considering their work rates and efficiencies.

Fundamental Concepts

Work Rate: If a person can complete a work in n days, their work rate is 1/n of the work per day.

Combined Work: If A can do work in a days and B can do it in b days, working together they complete (1/a + 1/b) of the work per day. Time taken together = 1 / (1/a + 1/b) = ab/(a+b) days

Example: A can complete work in 10 days, B in 15 days. Together: 1/10 + 1/15 = 3/30 + 2/30 = 5/30 = 1/6 per day. Time = 6 days

Work and Wages

Wages are distributed in the ratio of work done. If A and B work together, their wage ratio equals their work ratio, which equals the ratio of (Efficiency × Time).

Example: A works for 6 days, B for 8 days. A's efficiency is twice B's. Work ratio = (2×6):(1×8) = 12:8 = 3:2. If total wage is $500, A gets $300, B gets $200.

Pipes and Cisterns

Similar to time-work problems, but involving filling or emptying tanks. Inlet pipes fill (positive work), outlet pipes empty (negative work).

Example: Pipe A fills tank in 12 hours, Pipe B empties in 18 hours. Net rate = 1/12 - 1/18 = 3/36 - 2/36 = 1/36 per hour. Time to fill = 36 hours

Efficiency Concepts

Efficiency is the rate of work. If A is twice as efficient as B, A completes work in half the time. Efficiency is inversely proportional to time: E₁/E₂ = T₂/T₁

3.3 Time, Speed, and Distance

These problems involve motion and are based on the fundamental relationship: Distance = Speed × Time

Basic Formulas

Distance = Speed × Time

Speed = Distance / Time

Time = Distance / Speed

Unit Conversions:

  • km/hr to m/s: Multiply by 5/18
  • m/s to km/hr: Multiply by 18/5

Example: 72 km/hr = 72 × 5/18 = 20 m/s

Average Speed

Average Speed = Total Distance / Total Time

Important: Average speed is NOT the average of speeds. If equal distances are covered at speeds s₁ and s₂, average speed = 2s₁s₂/(s₁+s₂) (harmonic mean)

Example: A person travels 60 km at 30 km/hr and returns at 20 km/hr. Average speed = 2×30×20/(30+20) = 1200/50 = 24 km/hr

Relative Speed

Same Direction: Relative speed = |Speed₁ - Speed₂|

Opposite Direction: Relative speed = Speed₁ + Speed₂

Example: Two trains traveling at 60 km/hr and 40 km/hr in opposite directions have relative speed = 100 km/hr

Trains Problems

When a train crosses a stationary object (pole, person), distance = length of train

When a train crosses a platform/bridge, distance = length of train + length of platform/bridge

When two trains cross each other, distance = sum of their lengths, speed = relative speed

Example: A 200m train crosses a 300m platform in 25 seconds. Speed = (200+300)/25 = 20 m/s = 72 km/hr

Boats and Streams

Downstream: Boat moves with current. Effective speed = Speed of boat + Speed of stream

Upstream: Boat moves against current. Effective speed = Speed of boat - Speed of stream

Formulas:

  • Speed of boat in still water = (Downstream speed + Upstream speed) / 2
  • Speed of stream = (Downstream speed - Upstream speed) / 2

Example: Boat travels 20 km downstream in 2 hours, returns in 4 hours. Downstream speed = 10 km/hr, Upstream speed = 5 km/hr. Boat speed = (10+5)/2 = 7.5 km/hr, Stream speed = (10-5)/2 = 2.5 km/hr

3.4 Summary and Key Takeaways

In this module, we have developed algebraic problem-solving skills and applied them to practical work and motion scenarios.

Critical Concepts to Remember:

  • Linear Equations can be solved by isolation; systems require substitution or elimination
  • Quadratic Equations have at most two solutions; discriminant determines their nature
  • Algebraic Identities enable quick expansion and factorization
  • Work Rate = 1/Time; combined work rates add algebraically
  • Efficiency is inversely proportional to time taken
  • Distance = Speed × Time is the fundamental motion relationship
  • Relative Speed depends on direction: add for opposite, subtract for same
  • Boats and Streams: Boat speed = average of downstream and upstream speeds
