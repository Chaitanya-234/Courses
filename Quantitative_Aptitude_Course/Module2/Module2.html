2.1 Percentages: Foundation of Proportional Reasoning

A percentage is a way of expressing a number as a fraction of 100. The word "percent" means "per hundred." Percentages provide a standardized way to compare proportions and changes, making them essential in finance, statistics, and everyday decision-making.

Basic Percentage Calculations

Converting Fractions to Percentages: Multiply the fraction by 100. Example: 3/4 = (3/4) × 100 = 75%

Converting Percentages to Fractions: Divide by 100 and simplify. Example: 60% = 60/100 = 3/5

Finding Percentage of a Number: Multiply the number by the percentage (as a decimal or fraction). Example: 25% of 80 = (25/100) × 80 = 20

Finding What Percentage One Number is of Another: Divide the first number by the second and multiply by 100. Example: What percent is 15 of 60? → (15/60) × 100 = 25%

Percentage Increase and Decrease

Percentage Increase: If a value increases from A to B, the percentage increase = [(B - A) / A] × 100

Example: If a price increases from $50 to $60, percentage increase = [(60 - 50) / 50] × 100 = 20%

Percentage Decrease: If a value decreases from A to B, the percentage decrease = [(A - B) / A] × 100

Example: If a price decreases from $80 to $64, percentage decrease = [(80 - 64) / 80] × 100 = 20%

Important Note: A 20% increase followed by a 20% decrease does NOT return to the original value. If original = 100, after 20% increase = 120, after 20% decrease from 120 = 96 (net 4% decrease).

Successive Percentage Changes

When multiple percentage changes are applied successively, they compound. The formula for two successive changes of a% and b% is:

Net change = a + b + (ab/100)%

Example: A 10% increase followed by a 20% increase → Net change = 10 + 20 + (10×20/100) = 10 + 20 + 2 = 32% increase

For a decrease, use negative values. Example: 20% increase followed by 10% decrease → Net change = 20 + (-10) + (20×(-10)/100) = 20 - 10 - 2 = 8% increase

2.2 Ratio and Proportion

A ratio is a comparison of two quantities by division. If two quantities A and B are in the ratio m:n, it means A/B = m/n, or A = (m/n) × B.

Ratio Basics

Simplifying Ratios: Divide both terms by their HCF. Example: 24:36 = 2:3 (dividing by HCF 12)

Equivalent Ratios: Multiplying or dividing both terms by the same number gives equivalent ratios. Example: 2:3 = 4:6 = 6:9

Comparing Ratios: Convert to fractions with common denominators or cross-multiply. Example: Which is greater, 3:4 or 5:7? → 3/4 = 21/28, 5/7 = 20/28, so 3:4 is greater.

Compound Ratios: If A:B = m:n and B:C = p:q, then A:B:C = mp:np:nq. Example: If A:B = 2:3 and B:C = 4:5, then A:B:C = 8:12:15

Direct and Inverse Proportion

Direct Proportion: Two quantities are directly proportional if an increase in one causes a proportional increase in the other. If A ∝ B, then A/B = constant, or A₁/B₁ = A₂/B₂.

Example: If 5 workers complete a task in 10 days, how many days will 10 workers take (assuming same efficiency)? This is inverse proportion: 5 × 10 = 10 × x → x = 5 days

Inverse Proportion: Two quantities are inversely proportional if an increase in one causes a proportional decrease in the other. If A ∝ 1/B, then A × B = constant, or A₁ × B₁ = A₂ × B₂.

Partnership Problems

In partnership problems, profit or loss is divided among partners in the ratio of their investments, considering both capital and time period.

Simple Partnership: When investments are for the same time period, profit is divided in the ratio of investments. Example: A invests $3000, B invests $5000. Profit ratio = 3:5

Compound Partnership: When investments are for different time periods, profit is divided in the ratio of (Capital × Time). Example: A invests $4000 for 6 months, B invests $6000 for 4 months. Profit ratio = (4000×6):(6000×4) = 24000:24000 = 1:1

2.3 Profit, Loss, and Discount

These concepts form the foundation of business mathematics and are essential for understanding commercial transactions.

Fundamental Concepts

Cost Price (CP): The price at which an article is purchased or manufactured.

Selling Price (SP): The price at which an article is sold.

Profit: When SP > CP, Profit = SP - CP

Loss: When CP > SP, Loss = CP - SP

Profit Percentage: (Profit / CP) × 100

Loss Percentage: (Loss / CP) × 100

Key Formulas

SP = CP + Profit = CP + (Profit% × CP / 100) = CP(1 + Profit%/100)

SP = CP - Loss = CP - (Loss% × CP / 100) = CP(1 - Loss%/100)

CP = SP / (1 + Profit%/100) when profit is made

CP = SP / (1 - Loss%/100) when loss is incurred

Marked Price and Discount

Marked Price (MP): The price marked on an article (list price) before any discount.

Discount: The reduction offered on the marked price. Discount = MP - SP

Discount Percentage: (Discount / MP) × 100

SP = MP - Discount = MP(1 - Discount%/100)

Successive Discounts

When multiple discounts are applied successively, use the successive percentage formula. For discounts of a% and b%:

Net discount = a + b - (ab/100)%

Example: Successive discounts of 20% and 10% → Net discount = 20 + 10 - (20×10/100) = 30 - 2 = 28%

Alternatively, apply discounts sequentially: After 20% discount, price = 0.8 × MP. After additional 10% discount, price = 0.9 × 0.8 × MP = 0.72 × MP (28% total discount)

2.4 Simple and Compound Interest

Interest is the cost of borrowing money or the return on lending/investing money. Understanding interest calculations is crucial for financial literacy and problem-solving.

Simple Interest (SI)

Simple interest is calculated only on the principal amount throughout the entire period.

Formula: SI = (P × R × T) / 100

Where: P = Principal (initial amount), R = Rate of interest per annum (%), T = Time period (years)

Amount: A = P + SI = P + (P × R × T / 100) = P(1 + RT/100)

Example: Principal = $1000, Rate = 5% per annum, Time = 3 years → SI = (1000 × 5 × 3) / 100 = $150, Amount = $1150

Compound Interest (CI)

Compound interest is calculated on the principal plus accumulated interest from previous periods. Interest earns interest.

Formula: A = P(1 + R/100)^T

CI = A - P = P[(1 + R/100)^T - 1]

Example: Principal = $1000, Rate = 5% per annum compounded annually, Time = 3 years → A = 1000(1.05)³ = 1000 × 1.157625 = $1157.63, CI = $157.63

Compounding Frequency

Interest can be compounded at different frequencies:

Annually: A = P(1 + R/100)^T

Semi-annually: A = P(1 + R/200)^(2T)

Quarterly: A = P(1 + R/400)^(4T)

Monthly: A = P(1 + R/1200)^(12T)

Difference Between SI and CI

For the same principal, rate, and time, CI > SI (except for the first period where they're equal).

The difference increases with time and rate. For 2 years: CI - SI = P(R/100)²

For 3 years: CI - SI = P(R/100)²(3 + R/100)

2.5 Summary and Key Takeaways

In this module, we have explored proportional reasoning and financial mathematics essential for business and everyday applications.

Critical Concepts to Remember:

  • Percentages provide standardized comparisons; successive percentage changes compound using the formula: a + b + (ab/100)%
  • Ratios compare quantities; in partnerships, profit is divided in the ratio of (Capital × Time)
  • Profit/Loss percentages are always calculated on Cost Price, not Selling Price
  • Successive Discounts are not additive; use the compounding formula or apply sequentially
  • Simple Interest is calculated only on principal; grows linearly
  • Compound Interest is calculated on principal plus accumulated interest; grows exponentially
  • CI > SI for periods greater than one, with the difference increasing over time
